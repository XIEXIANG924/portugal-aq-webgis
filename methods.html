Methods & Data

Data sources, processing pipeline, and technical infrastructure

Data Sources

CAMS Reanalysis

CAMS Reanalysis provides gridded atmospheric composition data, including NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀ concentrations. In this project, it was used to generate monthly and annual pollutant maps for Portugal and to calculate air quality changes between 2021 and 2023.

Copernicus Land Cover

Copernicus Land Cover data describe the spatial distribution of land cover classes such as cropland, forest, urban areas, water, and shrubland. The 2021 and 2023 land cover maps were compared to detect stable, gain, and loss areas and to analyze how land cover transitions relate to pollutant changes.

WorldPop 2023

WorldPop 2023 provides population count data in raster format, showing the spatial distribution of population. It was used to estimate population exposure by overlaying population data with pollutant concentration classes and calculating the number of people living in each air quality level.

GAUL Boundaries

GAUL Boundaries provide administrative units used to aggregate and summarize spatial data. In this project, they were used for zonal statistics, bivariate analysis, and population exposure assessment at the administrative-unit level.

Processing Pipeline

Pollutant Maps — December 2023

Raw CAMS NetCDF files containing hourly pollutant data were processed with GDAL to calculate monthly mean concentrations for NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀. The resulting raster layers were clipped to the Portugal study area and prepared for further analysis.

Annual Average Maps — 2023

For each pollutant, the twelve monthly raster layers were clipped to the study boundary and aggregated using the average operator. The annual mean concentration was calculated as Pyear(x, y) = 1/12 × Σ Pmonth(x, y), where P represents NO₂, PM₂.₅, or PM₁₀ concentration at each raster cell.

Concentration Classification

The annual average concentration maps were reclassified into five air quality classes according to EU annual concentration thresholds. Each pollutant was classified separately, from low concentration levels to very high concentration levels. The classified maps were styled with SLD and published through GeoServer as WMS layers.

AMAC — Annual Mean Aggregation Change

The change in annual mean pollutant concentration between 2021 and 2023 was calculated using raster subtraction: AMACP(x, y) = P2023(x, y) − P2021(x, y). Negative values indicate a decrease in pollutant concentration, while positive values indicate an increase. The AMAC maps for NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀ are the core layers of the WebGIS.

Land Cover Change

The 2021 and 2023 ESRI 10 m Land Cover maps were compared to identify stable, gain, and loss areas for each land cover class. Land cover transitions were encoded as LCC(x, y) = 100 × LC2021(x, y) + LC2023(x, y). For each target class c, change categories were defined as Stable, Gain, and Loss based on comparing 2021 and 2023 class values. Area statistics were calculated in EPSG:3035 to obtain more accurate area-based percentages.

Zonal Statistics

Land cover change categories were used as zones to extract pollutant change values from the AMAC rasters. For each pollutant and each land cover transition category, mean, minimum, and maximum AMAC values were calculated: Mean_AMACz = 1/nz × Σ AMACP(x, y). This step allows the comparison of pollutant changes across stable, gain, and loss land cover areas.

Bivariate Analysis

Population exposure and pollutant concentration were combined at administrative-unit level. Population density was classified into three levels, and pollutant concentration was also grouped into three levels. The bivariate class was created by combining pop_class_median and pol_class_max. The result is a 3 × 3 bivariate classification showing where higher population exposure overlaps with higher pollution levels.

Population Exposure

Population exposure was calculated by overlaying the 2023 population raster with pollutant concentration classes. For each air quality class k, the total exposed population was computed as PopSumk = Σ Population(x, y) for all pixels in class k. The exposure share was then calculated as ExposureSharek = PopSumk / Σ PopSumall × 100. The results are visualized with doughnut charts showing the proportion of population exposed to each pollutant concentration level.

Technology Stack

QGIS 3.40 + GDAL

QGIS and GDAL were used as the main geospatial processing tools. The workflow included raster clipping, reprojection, resampling, raster calculation, reclassification by table, and annual aggregation using GRASS r.series.

GeoServer (OGC WMS)

The Polimi Online GeoServer was used to publish the main raster and vector outputs as WMS layers, including AMAC maps, annual average pollutant maps, concentration classification maps, land cover change, and bivariate layers. SLD styles were assigned to the layers to ensure consistent visualization and legend generation.

OpenLayers 10

OpenLayers was used to develop the interactive WebGIS interface. The map includes two base maps, grouped layer switching, GeoServer WMS overlays, dynamic legends, Scale Line, Full Screen, and Mouse Position controls.

GitHub Pages

GitHub Pages was used to host the static website, including the Home, Methods, Results, and WebGIS pages. No backend server is required, since all spatial layers are served externally through GeoServer WMS.
