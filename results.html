1. Results Overview

This section presents the main outputs of the Portugal air quality analysis, including pollutant changes between 2021 and 2023, 2023 concentration classification maps, land cover change statistics, zonal statistics, bivariate exposure analysis, and population exposure charts. Together, these results show how NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀ concentrations vary across the country and how these patterns relate to land cover transitions and population distribution.

5. Zonal Statistics

This section presents the zonal statistics results, showing the mean, minimum, and maximum pollutant concentration changes for selected land cover change categories, including stable, gain, and loss areas.

For Crops, PM₂.₅ shows negative mean values across stable, gain, and loss areas, indicating an overall decrease in concentration. The minimum values are strongly negative, while the maximum values remain slightly positive, suggesting local variation within each change category.

For Built Area, NO₂ shows small negative mean values but a wide range between minimum and maximum values. This indicates that although the average change is slightly decreasing, some built-up areas experienced local increases in NO₂ concentration.

For Trees, PM₁₀ presents consistently negative mean values across stable, gain, and loss areas. This suggests a general decrease in PM₁₀ concentration in tree-related land cover zones, although maximum values show that some localized increases are still present.

8. Key Findings

Land Cover Stability and Main Transition Patterns

The land cover change statistics show that Trees, Built Area, and Crops remained largely stable between 2021 and 2023, with persistence values of 82.59%, 93.46%, and 88.62% respectively. Built Area shows the highest stability, indicating limited urban land transformation, while Trees and Crops also remain mostly persistent. Most gains and losses are mainly linked to exchanges with rangeland, which is the dominant source of gain and destination of loss for both Trees and Crops. For Built Area, new urban areas mainly came from Trees, Rangeland, and Crops, while losses mainly transitioned back to Rangeland, Trees, and Crops. Overall, the results suggest that the study area experienced moderate land cover transitions but no major structural land cover change during the 2021–2023 period.

Population Exposure by Pollutant Class

The exposure charts show that PM₂.₅ has the largest share of residents in the moderate class, with 88.1% of the population exposed to moderate PM₂.₅ levels. For NO₂, most residents are in the good class (55.3%), while PM₁₀ shows a more balanced pattern but still has a higher share in the moderate class (59.1%). Overall, PM₂.₅ appears to be the most critical pollutant in terms of population exposure in 2023.

Pollutant Change by Land Cover Category

The zonal statistics indicate that most selected land cover categories experienced an overall decrease in pollutant concentration between 2021 and 2023, especially for PM₂.₅ in Crops and PM₁₀ in Trees. However, the presence of positive maximum values shows that improvements were not uniform across all areas, and some local zones still recorded pollutant increases.
